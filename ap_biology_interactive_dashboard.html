Unit 1: Chemistry of Life

8-11%Structure/function
Water Properties
  • Hydrogen bonding gives water cohesion, adhesion, high specific heat, high heat of vaporization, and lower density as ice.
  • AP-level answer pattern: hydrogen bonds → water property → biological consequence.

Protein Structure & Enzymes
  • Primary structure: amino acid sequence.
  • Secondary: alpha helices and beta sheets.
  • Tertiary: overall 3D shape.
  • Quaternary: multiple polypeptide subunits.
  • Enzymes lower activation energy but do not change ΔG, equilibrium, or whether a reaction is spontaneous.
  • Temperature, pH, inhibitors, and substrate concentration affect enzyme activity.
Common trap: High temperature can disrupt hydrogen bonds and ionic interactions, changing tertiary structure and altering the active site.

Surface Area-to-Volume Ratio

As cell size increases, volume grows faster than surface area. This limits exchange of nutrients, gases, and wastes. Adaptations such as folds, villi, and flattened shapes increase exchange efficiency.

Unit 3: Cellular Energetics

12-16%Very high yield
ATP & Energy Coupling

ATP hydrolysis releases usable energy that can be coupled to cellular work such as active transport, movement, and biosynthesis.

Cellular Respiration
  1. Glycolysis: cytoplasm; glucose split; small ATP gain; NADH produced; does not require oxygen.
  2. Pyruvate oxidation: pyruvate enters mitochondrion; CO₂ released; acetyl-CoA formed.
  3. Krebs cycle: matrix; CO₂, NADH, FADH₂, small ATP/GTP produced.
  4. ETC/oxidative phosphorylation: inner mitochondrial membrane; electrons move through proteins; protons pumped; ATP synthase makes ATP; oxygen is final electron acceptor.
Photosynthesis
  1. Light reactions: thylakoid membrane; water split; oxygen released; ATP and NADPH produced.
  2. Calvin cycle: stroma; CO₂ fixed into sugars using ATP and NADPH.
Common trap: Plants perform both photosynthesis and cellular respiration.
Experimental Angles
  • Light intensity, wavelength, CO₂ concentration, temperature, and leaf disk flotation can be used to measure photosynthesis rate.
  • Oxygen consumption or CO₂ production can estimate respiration rate.
  • Enzyme rate often appears as slope of product formation over time.

Unit 4: Cell Communication & Cell Cycle

10-15%Systems interactions
Signal Transduction
  1. Reception: ligand binds receptor.
  2. Transduction: intracellular pathway relays and amplifies signal.
  3. Response: gene expression, enzyme activation, transport change, secretion, movement, or division.

Signals can be local, long-distance endocrine, autocrine, or direct cell-to-cell.

Feedback
  • Negative feedback: response reduces original stimulus; maintains homeostasis.
  • Positive feedback: response amplifies stimulus; useful for rapid completion events.
Cell Cycle & Cancer

Cell cycle: G1 → S → G2 → M. Checkpoints regulate progression. Cyclins and CDKs control timing. Cancer results from loss of regulation, often involving proto-oncogenes, oncogenes, tumor suppressor genes, and apoptosis pathways.

Unit 5: Heredity

8-11%Probability heavy
Meiosis
  • Meiosis reduces chromosome number from diploid to haploid.
  • Crossing over occurs in prophase I and creates recombinant chromosomes.
  • Independent assortment occurs as homologous pairs align randomly.
  • Random fertilization further increases variation.
Inheritance Patterns
  • Complete dominance, incomplete dominance, codominance, multiple alleles, polygenic traits.
  • Sex-linked traits often appear more frequently in males when recessive and X-linked.
  • Linked genes are inherited together more often unless crossing over separates them.
Probability
  • Product rule = probability of event A AND event B.
  • Sum rule = probability of event A OR event B.

Unit 6: Gene Expression & Regulation

12-16%Very high yield
DNA, Replication, and Central Dogma
  • DNA strands are antiparallel and complementary.
  • Replication is semi-conservative.
  • Central dogma: DNA → RNA → protein.
  • Transcription makes RNA from DNA; translation uses mRNA codons to build polypeptides.
Gene Regulation
  • Prokaryotes use operons to regulate transcription efficiently.
  • lac operon: inducible; activated when lactose is present and glucose is low.
  • trp operon: repressible; turned off when tryptophan is abundant.
  • Eukaryotic regulation includes transcription factors, chromatin modification, RNA processing, and post-translational modification.
Mutations & Biotechnology
  • Silent, missense, nonsense, insertion, deletion, frameshift.
  • Mutation effects depend on location and whether protein structure/function changes.
  • PCR amplifies DNA, gel electrophoresis separates DNA by size, CRISPR edits DNA, sequencing determines nucleotide order.

Unit 7: Natural Selection

13-20%Highest yield
Evolution & Natural Selection
  • Evolution is change in allele frequencies in a population over generations.
  • Natural selection requires heritable variation, differential survival/reproduction, and environmental selection pressure.
  • Fitness means reproductive success, not strength.
Mechanisms of Evolution
MechanismEffect
MutationIntroduces new alleles.
Natural selectionNonrandom increase in traits that improve fitness in an environment.
Genetic driftRandom allele frequency changes, strongest in small populations.
Gene flowAlleles move between populations.
Nonrandom matingChanges genotype frequencies.
Hardy-Weinberg

Hardy-Weinberg is a null model. If allele or genotype frequencies deviate from expectations, evolution may be occurring.

Units 8-9: Ecology, Biodiversity & Evolutionary Relationships

Combined final sectionSystems + evolution
Population Ecology
  • Exponential growth occurs under ideal conditions with unlimited resources.
  • Logistic growth slows as the population approaches carrying capacity.
  • Density-dependent factors include competition, predation, disease, and resource limitation.
  • Density-independent factors include weather, natural disasters, and seasonal changes.
Community & Ecosystem Ecology
  • Energy flows through trophic levels; matter cycles.
  • Only a fraction of energy transfers between trophic levels, commonly approximated by the 10% rule.
  • Interactions include competition, predation, mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism.
  • Keystone species and invasive species can strongly alter community structure.
Biogeochemical Cycles
  • Carbon cycle: photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, combustion.
  • Nitrogen cycle: fixation, nitrification, assimilation, ammonification, denitrification.
  • Water cycle: evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration.
Biodiversity & Phylogeny
  • Phylogenetic trees show hypotheses about evolutionary relationships.
  • Nodes represent common ancestors; closer branching can indicate more recent common ancestry.
  • Shared derived traits define clades.
  • Speciation can be allopatric or sympatric and depends on reproductive isolation.
  • Prezygotic barriers prevent fertilization; postzygotic barriers reduce hybrid viability or fertility.

Quantitative Practice: Hardy-Weinberg, Chi-Square & Graphs

Must practiceCommon FRQ skill
Hardy-Weinberg Quick Guide
p + q = 1
p² + 2pq + q² = 1

p = dominant allele frequency, q = recessive allele frequency, p² = homozygous dominant, 2pq = heterozygous, q² = homozygous recessive.

Practice 1: 9% of a population shows a recessive trait. Find q, p, and percent heterozygous.
q² = 0.09, so q = 0.30. p = 0.70. 2pq = 2(0.70)(0.30) = 0.42, so 42% are heterozygous.
Practice 2: In a population, p = 0.7. Find q, q², and 2pq.
q = 0.3. q² = 0.09, so 9% are homozygous recessive. 2pq = 2(0.7)(0.3) = 0.42, so 42% are heterozygous.
Practice 3: 25 of 400 individuals show a recessive phenotype. Calculate q², q, p, and 2pq.
q² = 25/400 = 0.0625. q = 0.25. p = 0.75. 2pq = 2(0.75)(0.25) = 0.375, so 37.5% are heterozygous.
Chi-Square Quick Guide
χ² = Σ [(O - E)² / E]
degrees of freedom = number of categories - 1

Use chi-square to determine whether observed results differ significantly from expected results. If χ² is greater than the critical value, reject the null hypothesis.

Practice 1: Expected 3:1 ratio in 100 offspring. Observed: 80 dominant, 20 recessive. Calculate χ².
Expected: 75 dominant, 25 recessive. χ² = (80-75)²/75 + (20-25)²/25 = 25/75 + 25/25 = 0.33 + 1 = 1.33. df = 1. If critical value is 3.84, fail to reject the null hypothesis.
Practice 2: A 9:3:3:1 cross produces 160 offspring. Observed: 88, 32, 30, 10. Calculate expected values and df.
Expected values: 90, 30, 30, 10. df = 4 - 1 = 3. χ² = (88-90)²/90 + (32-30)²/30 + (30-30)²/30 + (10-10)²/10 = 4/90 + 4/30 + 0 + 0 = 0.044 + 0.133 = 0.177.
Graph Analysis & Rates
rate = Δy / Δx

Always describe the trend with numbers and biological meaning. Strong answer: “From 0 to 4 minutes, oxygen production increased from 0 mL to 25 mL, indicating an increasing photosynthetic rate under the tested conditions.”

Practice: Oxygen production: 0 min = 0 mL, 2 min = 10 mL, 4 min = 25 mL. Calculate the rate from 0-2 min and 2-4 min.
0-2 min: (10-0)/(2-0) = 5 mL/min. 2-4 min: (25-10)/(4-2) = 7.5 mL/min. The rate increased, possibly because conditions favored greater photosynthetic output during the later interval.

FRQ Practice Center

Write to score points
FRQ formula: Answer the exact verb. Describe = what happens. Explain = why/how it happens. Justify = connect evidence to claim. Predict = state expected outcome.

FRQ 1: Enzymes & Energetics

A graph shows enzyme activity increasing from 10°C to 37°C and decreasing sharply above 45°C.

  1. Describe the trend.
  2. Explain why activity decreases at high temperature.
  3. Predict the effect of changing pH far from the optimum.
  4. Justify your prediction using protein structure.

FRQ 2: Gene Expression

A point mutation changes a codon in a gene that codes for an enzyme.

  1. Describe transcription and translation.
  2. Explain how the mutation could affect protein function.
  3. Predict one possible phenotypic effect.
  4. Justify using structure-function reasoning.

FRQ 3: Evolution

A bacterial population becomes increasingly resistant to an antibiotic after repeated exposure.

  1. Explain how natural selection leads to resistance.
  2. Describe the role of genetic variation.
  3. Predict the change in allele frequency.
  4. Justify the prediction using evolutionary principles.

FRQ 4: Cell Transport

A plant cell is placed in a hypertonic solution.

  1. Describe water movement.
  2. Explain the effect on the cell.
  3. Predict the long-term outcome.
  4. Justify using membrane properties and osmosis.

FRQ 5: Ecology + Evolution

A drought reduces available food and water in an ecosystem. A small mammal population declines sharply.

  1. Explain how carrying capacity changes.
  2. Describe how population growth is affected.
  3. Predict how natural selection may act on the population.
  4. Justify using ecology and evolution together.

Final Exam Strategy

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Saying individuals evolve instead of populations.
  • Forgetting that fitness means reproductive success.
  • Saying enzymes change equilibrium or ΔG.
  • Forgetting plants perform cellular respiration.
  • Not using units in calculations.
  • Describing graphs vaguely without numbers.
  • Writing definitions instead of mechanisms.

High-Scoring Sentence Stems

  • “The data support the claim because...”
  • “As ___ increases, ___ changes because...”
  • “This change would increase fitness by...”
  • “The mutation may alter protein function because...”
  • “Water moves from ___ to ___ because...”
  • “The control group is necessary because...”

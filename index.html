Core AP Biology mindset: The exam tests mechanisms and reasoning more than memorized definitions. Students should explain how structures produce functions, how energy and matter move through systems, how information is stored and expressed, and how evolution explains unity and diversity.

Unit 1: Chemistry of Life

8-11%Structure/function
Water Properties
  • Hydrogen bonding gives water cohesion, adhesion, high specific heat, high heat of vaporization, and lower density as ice.
  • AP answer pattern: hydrogen bonds → water property → biological consequence.
Macromolecules

LipidsNot true polymersMembranes, long-term energy, hormonesPhospholipids are amphipathic
ProteinsAmino acidsEnzymes, transport, signaling, structureR-group chemistry drives folding and function
Nucleic acidsNucleotidesStore and transmit informationDNA/RNA structure supports information flow
Protein Structure & Enzymes
  • Primary: amino acid sequence.
  • Secondary: alpha helices and beta sheets.
  • Tertiary: overall 3D shape.
  • Quaternary: multiple subunits.
  • Enzymes lower activation energy but do not change ΔG, equilibrium, or whether a reaction is spontaneous.
Common trap: High temperature can disrupt bonds, changing tertiary structure and altering the active site.

Surface Area-to-Volume Ratio

As cell size increases, volume grows faster than surface area. This limits exchange. Folds, villi, and flattened shapes increase exchange efficiency.

Unit 3: Cellular Energetics

12-16%Very high yield
ATP & Energy Coupling

ATP hydrolysis releases usable energy that can be coupled to active transport, movement, and biosynthesis.

Cellular Respiration
  1. Glycolysis: cytoplasm; glucose split; ATP and NADH produced; no oxygen required.
  2. Pyruvate oxidation: CO₂ released; acetyl-CoA formed.
  3. Krebs cycle: matrix; CO₂, NADH, FADH₂, small ATP/GTP produced.
  4. ETC: inner mitochondrial membrane; protons pumped; ATP synthase makes ATP; oxygen is final electron acceptor.
Photosynthesis
  1. Light reactions: thylakoid membrane; water split; O₂ released; ATP and NADPH produced.
  2. Calvin cycle: stroma; CO₂ fixed into sugars.
Common trap: Plants perform both photosynthesis and cellular respiration.

Unit 4: Cell Communication & Cell Cycle

10-15%Systems interactions
Signal Transduction
  1. Reception: ligand binds receptor.
  2. Transduction: intracellular pathway relays and amplifies signal.
  3. Response: gene expression, enzyme activation, transport change, secretion, movement, or division.
Feedback
  • Negative feedback: response reduces original stimulus.
  • Positive feedback: response amplifies stimulus.
Cell Cycle & Cancer

Cell cycle: G1 → S → G2 → M. Checkpoints regulate progression. Cancer results from loss of regulation, often involving proto-oncogenes, oncogenes, tumor suppressor genes, and apoptosis pathways.

Unit 5: Heredity

8-11%Probability heavy
Meiosis
  • Meiosis reduces chromosome number from diploid to haploid.
  • Crossing over occurs in prophase I.
  • Independent assortment occurs as homologous pairs align randomly.
  • Random fertilization further increases variation.
Inheritance Patterns
  • Complete dominance, incomplete dominance, codominance, multiple alleles, polygenic traits.
  • Sex-linked traits often appear more frequently in males when recessive and X-linked.
  • Linked genes are inherited together more often unless crossing over separates them.

Unit 6: Gene Expression & Regulation

12-16%Very high yield
DNA, Replication, and Central Dogma
  • DNA strands are antiparallel and complementary.
  • Replication is semi-conservative.
  • Central dogma: DNA → RNA → protein.
  • Transcription makes RNA from DNA; translation uses mRNA codons to build polypeptides.
Gene Regulation
  • lac operon: inducible; activated when lactose is present and glucose is low.
  • trp operon: repressible; turned off when tryptophan is abundant.
  • Eukaryotic regulation includes transcription factors, chromatin modification, RNA processing, and post-translational modification.
Mutations & Biotechnology
  • Silent, missense, nonsense, insertion, deletion, frameshift.
  • PCR amplifies DNA, gel electrophoresis separates DNA by size, CRISPR edits DNA.

Unit 7: Natural Selection

13-20%Highest yield
Evolution & Natural Selection
  • Evolution is change in allele frequencies in a population over generations.
  • Natural selection requires heritable variation, differential survival/reproduction, and environmental selection pressure.
  • Fitness means reproductive success, not strength.
Mechanisms of Evolution
MechanismEffect
MutationIntroduces new alleles.
Natural selectionNonrandom increase in traits that improve fitness.
Genetic driftRandom allele frequency changes, strongest in small populations.
Gene flowAlleles move between populations.
Hardy-Weinberg

Hardy-Weinberg is a null model. If allele or genotype frequencies deviate from expectations, evolution may be occurring.

p + q = 1
p² + 2pq + q² = 1

Units 8-9: Ecology, Biodiversity & Evolutionary Relationships

Combined final sectionSystems + evolution
Population Ecology
  • Exponential growth occurs under ideal conditions with unlimited resources.
  • Logistic growth slows as population approaches carrying capacity.
  • Density-dependent factors include competition, predation, disease, and resource limitation.
  • Density-independent factors include weather, disasters, and seasonal changes.
Community & Ecosystem Ecology
  • Energy flows through trophic levels; matter cycles.
  • Only a fraction of energy transfers between trophic levels.
  • Interactions include competition, predation, mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism.
Biodiversity & Phylogeny
  • Nodes represent common ancestors.
  • Shared derived traits define clades.
  • Speciation can be allopatric or sympatric.
  • Prezygotic barriers prevent fertilization; postzygotic barriers reduce hybrid viability or fertility.

Quantitative Practice

Practice: 9% show a recessive trait. Find q, p, and percent heterozygous.
q² = 0.09, q = 0.30, p = 0.70, 2pq = 0.42 = 42% heterozygous.
Chi-Square
χ² = Σ [(O - E)² / E]
df = categories - 1
Practice: Expected 3:1 in 100 offspring. Observed: 80 dominant, 20 recessive. Calculate χ².
Expected 75 and 25. χ² = 25/75 + 25/25 = 1.33. df = 1. If critical value is 3.84, fail to reject the null.

FRQ Practice Center

Write to score pointsCED-style prompts
FRQ formula: Answer the exact verb. Describe = what happens. Explain = why/how it happens. Justify = connect evidence to claim. Predict = state expected outcome. Calculate = show setup, answer, and units when needed.
How to Score More FRQ Points
  • Label responses clearly as a, b, c, d.
  • Use biological vocabulary: allele frequency, active site, concentration gradient, signal transduction, carrying capacity.
  • Do not just name a process; explain the mechanism.
  • When data are provided, cite the data directly.
  • For experimental design, include independent variable, dependent variable, control, constants, and expected result.

FRQ 1: Enzymes & Energetics

A graph shows enzyme activity increasing from 10°C to 37°C and decreasing sharply above 45°C.

  1. Describe the trend shown in the graph.
  2. Explain why activity decreases at high temperature.
  3. Predict the effect of changing pH far from the optimum.
  4. Justify your prediction using protein structure.
Scoring guide: Award points for identifying the optimum temperature, explaining denaturation/changed active site, predicting reduced activity at nonoptimal pH, and connecting disrupted bonds/tertiary structure to reduced substrate binding.

FRQ 2: Cellular Respiration Data

A researcher measures oxygen consumption in germinating seeds at 10°C, 22°C, and 35°C.

  1. Identify the dependent variable.
  2. Explain why oxygen consumption can be used as a measure of cellular respiration.
  3. Predict which temperature will likely show the highest respiration rate.
  4. Justify your prediction using enzyme activity and metabolism.
Scoring guide: DV = oxygen consumption/rate. Oxygen is final electron acceptor in the ETC. Prediction should be supported by temperature effects on enzymes; extreme temperatures may reduce activity by disrupting protein structure.

FRQ 3: Photosynthesis Experiment

Students use floating leaf disks to test photosynthesis rate under different light intensities.

  1. Identify the independent variable.
  2. Identify one appropriate control.
  3. Explain why disks float as photosynthesis occurs.
  4. Predict how increasing light intensity affects the rate until another factor becomes limiting.
Scoring guide: IV = light intensity. Control could be dark/no light or constant CO₂/temperature. Disks float because O₂ accumulates in leaf tissue. Rate increases until another limiting factor such as CO₂ or enzyme capacity limits photosynthesis.

FRQ 4: Cell Transport & Osmosis

A plant cell is placed in a hypertonic solution.

  1. Describe the direction of net water movement.
  2. Explain the effect on the plant cell.
  3. Predict the long-term outcome if the cell remains in the solution.
  4. Justify using membrane properties and water potential/osmosis.
Scoring guide: Water leaves the cell toward the higher solute concentration. The cell loses turgor pressure and plasmolyzes. Explanation should include selectively permeable membrane and osmosis down water potential gradient.

FRQ 5: Cell Communication

A mutation changes the shape of a membrane receptor involved in a hormone signaling pathway.

  1. Describe the normal sequence of a signal transduction pathway.
  2. Explain how the receptor mutation could affect the pathway.
  3. Predict one possible cellular response if the ligand cannot bind.
  4. Justify your prediction using structure-function reasoning.
Scoring guide: Normal pathway: reception, transduction, response. Altered receptor shape can prevent ligand binding or activation. Cellular response may be reduced/absent because signal is not transmitted or amplified.

FRQ 6: Cell Cycle & Cancer

A mutation in a tumor suppressor gene prevents a checkpoint protein from functioning.

  1. Describe the role of checkpoints in the cell cycle.
  2. Explain how loss of checkpoint function can lead to cancer.
  3. Predict how apoptosis could affect damaged cells.
  4. Justify why mutations in regulatory genes can have large effects.
Scoring guide: Checkpoints prevent division of damaged cells. Loss allows uncontrolled division and mutation accumulation. Apoptosis eliminates damaged cells. Regulatory genes affect many downstream cell cycle events.

FRQ 7: Meiosis & Heredity

Two genes are located on the same chromosome. Offspring phenotypes do not match the expected independent assortment ratio.

  1. Explain why linked genes may not assort independently.
  2. Describe how crossing over can produce recombinant offspring.
  3. Predict how distance between genes affects recombination frequency.
  4. Justify your prediction.
Scoring guide: Linked genes are inherited together because they are on the same chromosome. Crossing over can separate alleles. Farther apart genes have higher recombination frequency because crossovers are more likely between them.

FRQ 8: Gene Expression & Mutation

A point mutation changes a codon in a gene that codes for an enzyme.

  1. Describe transcription and translation.
  2. Explain how the mutation could affect protein function.
  3. Predict one possible phenotypic effect.
  4. Justify using structure-function reasoning.
Scoring guide: Transcription makes mRNA from DNA; translation uses codons to assemble amino acids. Mutation may change amino acid sequence, folding, active site, and enzyme function; phenotype depends on protein role.

FRQ 9: Gene Regulation

Bacteria are grown in a medium containing lactose but no glucose.

  1. Predict whether the lac operon will be active.
  2. Explain the role of lactose/allolactose in regulation.
  3. Explain why absence of glucose increases lac operon transcription.
  4. Justify how this regulation benefits the cell.
Scoring guide: lac operon active. Allolactose binds repressor, preventing it from blocking transcription. Low glucose increases cAMP-CAP activation. This allows efficient lactose metabolism only when useful.

FRQ 10: Hardy-Weinberg

In a population of 500 individuals, 45 individuals show a recessive phenotype.

  1. Calculate q².
  2. Calculate q and p.
  3. Calculate the expected frequency of heterozygotes.
  4. Identify one condition required for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.
Scoring guide: q² = 45/500 = 0.09. q = 0.30. p = 0.70. 2pq = 2(0.70)(0.30) = 0.42. Conditions include no selection, no mutation, no migration, large population, or random mating.

FRQ 11: Natural Selection

A bacterial population becomes increasingly resistant to an antibiotic after repeated exposure.

  1. Explain how natural selection leads to resistance.
  2. Describe the role of genetic variation.
  3. Predict the change in allele frequency over time.
  4. Justify using evolutionary principles.
Scoring guide: Resistant variants survive/reproduce more under antibiotic pressure. Variation may arise by mutation. Resistance allele frequency increases because resistant individuals have higher fitness in that environment.

FRQ 12: Ecology + Evolution

A drought reduces available food and water in an ecosystem. A small mammal population declines sharply.

  1. Explain how carrying capacity changes.
  2. Describe how population growth is affected.
  3. Predict how natural selection may act on the population.
  4. Justify using ecology and evolution together.
Scoring guide: Carrying capacity decreases due to reduced resources. Population growth slows or becomes negative. Traits improving survival/reproduction during drought may increase. Ecology creates selection pressure that drives evolutionary change.

FRQ 13: Chi-Square Genetics

A monohybrid cross is expected to produce a 3:1 phenotype ratio. The observed offspring are 80 dominant and 20 recessive.

  1. Calculate the expected numbers for 100 offspring.
  2. Calculate χ².
  3. Identify degrees of freedom.
  4. Explain whether the results support the expected ratio if the critical value is 3.84.
Scoring guide: Expected 75 and 25. χ² = (80-75)²/75 + (20-25)²/25 = 1.33. df = 1. Since 1.33 < 3.84, fail to reject the null; results are consistent with expected ratio.

FRQ 14: Cladograms & Biodiversity

A cladogram shows species A and B sharing a more recent common ancestor with each other than with species C.

  1. Identify which two species are most closely related.
  2. Explain what a node represents.
  3. Describe how shared derived traits are used in cladograms.
  4. Justify why DNA sequence comparisons can support evolutionary relationships.
Scoring guide: A and B are most closely related. A node represents a common ancestor. Shared derived traits define clades. More similar DNA sequences suggest more recent common ancestry.

Final Exam Strategy

Common Mistakes

  • Saying individuals evolve instead of populations.
  • Forgetting fitness means reproductive success.
  • Saying enzymes change equilibrium or ΔG.
  • Forgetting plants perform respiration.
  • Not using units in calculations.

High-Scoring Stems

  • “The data support the claim because...”
  • “As ___ increases, ___ changes because...”
  • “This change would increase fitness by...”
  • “Water moves from ___ to ___ because...”

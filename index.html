Unit 1: Chemistry of Life

8–11%High-yield
Big Ideas (CED)
  • Structure determines function at the molecular level.
  • Water properties support life because of hydrogen bonding.
  • Biological polymers are built and broken by dehydration synthesis and hydrolysis.
Water: Properties & Biological Importance
  • Polarity: Water is polar; oxygen is partially negative and hydrogens are partially positive.
  • Hydrogen bonding: Causes cohesion, adhesion, high specific heat, evaporative cooling, and ice being less dense than liquid water.
  • Solvent properties: Water dissolves many ionic and polar substances, enabling transport and chemical reactions.
  • Plant transport: Cohesion and adhesion help move water through xylem.
Macromolecules: Structure → Function
MoleculeKey structureFunctionCED-style connection
CarbohydratesMonosaccharides; glycosidic bondsEnergy and structural supportStarch/glycogen store energy; cellulose provides plant structure.
LipidsHydrophobic; phospholipids are amphipathicMembranes, hormones, long-term energyHydrophobic interactions drive membrane formation.
ProteinsAmino acids; peptide bonds; R-groupsEnzymes, transport, signaling, structureR-group chemistry affects folding and function.
Nucleic acidsNucleotides; sugar-phosphate backbone; base pairingStore and transmit genetic informationComplementary base pairing supports replication and expression.
Protein Structure & Enzymes
  • Primary: amino acid sequence.
  • Secondary: alpha helices and beta sheets from hydrogen bonding.
  • Tertiary: overall 3D shape from R-group interactions.
  • Quaternary: multiple polypeptide subunits.
  • Enzymes lower activation energy but do not change ΔG, equilibrium, or whether a reaction is spontaneous.
  • Temperature, pH, inhibitors, and substrate concentration affect enzyme activity.
Common CED trap: Denaturation changes protein shape, which can change the active site and reduce function.
Common Mistakes
  • Saying lipids are polymers in the same way as proteins or nucleic acids.
  • Forgetting that R-groups determine amino acid properties.
  • Saying enzymes “add energy” instead of lowering activation energy.
FRQ Tip: Connect molecular structure → interaction → biological function.

Unit 2: Cell Structure & Function

10–13%FRQ heavy
Big Ideas (CED)
  • Cell structures are specialized for specific functions.
  • Membranes regulate exchange with the environment.
  • Surface area-to-volume ratio limits cell size and affects transport efficiency.
Cell Structures & Endomembrane System
StructureFunction
NucleusStores DNA; transcription occurs here in eukaryotes.
RibosomesBuild proteins by translation.
Rough ERProcesses proteins for membranes/secretion.
Smooth ERLipid synthesis, detoxification, calcium storage.
Golgi apparatusModifies, sorts, and ships proteins/lipids.
Lysosomes/vacuolesDigestion, recycling, storage, water balance.
Mitochondria/chloroplastsEnergy transformation; both contain internal membranes.
Membranes & Transport
  • Phospholipid bilayer is selectively permeable.
  • Small nonpolar molecules cross most easily; ions and polar molecules need proteins.
  • Passive transport: diffusion, facilitated diffusion, osmosis.
  • Active transport: requires energy to move substances against gradients.
  • Bulk transport: endocytosis and exocytosis move large materials.
Tonicity & Water Potential
  • Hypertonic solution → water leaves cell.
  • Hypotonic solution → water enters cell.
  • Water moves from higher water potential to lower water potential.
  • Solutes lower water potential.
Common mistake: Students often reverse water movement. Water moves toward higher solute concentration.
Surface Area-to-Volume Ratio
  • As cells get larger, volume increases faster than surface area.
  • Low SA:V makes exchange less efficient.
  • Adaptations like folds, microvilli, and flattened shapes increase exchange.
FRQ Tip: For transport questions, name the gradient, direction of movement, membrane component, and cellular consequence.

Unit 3: Cellular Energetics

12–16%MOST TESTED
Big Ideas (CED)
  • Energy is transformed, not created or destroyed.
  • Life depends on energy coupling and enzyme-catalyzed reactions.
  • Photosynthesis and cellular respiration use electron transfer and proton gradients.
Enzymes & Reaction Rates
  • Enzymes lower activation energy.
  • Substrate concentration increases rate until active sites are saturated.
  • Competitive inhibitors bind active sites; noncompetitive inhibitors alter enzyme shape.
  • Temperature and pH affect protein structure and enzyme function.
Cellular Respiration
  • Glycolysis: cytoplasm; produces ATP, NADH, and pyruvate.
  • Pyruvate oxidation: pyruvate becomes acetyl-CoA; CO₂ released.
  • Krebs cycle: produces CO₂, NADH, FADH₂, and small ATP/GTP.
  • ETC: electrons power proton pumping; oxygen is final electron acceptor.
  • Chemiosmosis: proton gradient powers ATP synthase.
  • Fermentation: regenerates NAD⁺ when oxygen is unavailable.
Photosynthesis
  • Light reactions: thylakoid membrane; water split; O₂ released; ATP and NADPH produced.
  • Calvin cycle: stroma; CO₂ fixed into sugars using ATP and NADPH.
  • Chlorophyll absorbs light energy; different wavelengths affect photosynthetic rate.
  • Plants also perform cellular respiration.
Common Data Questions
  • Calculate rate from slope.
  • Explain how temperature/light/CO₂ affects rate.
  • Interpret oxygen production or consumption as evidence of photosynthesis or respiration.
FRQ Tip: Explain energy flow using electron movement, proton gradients, and ATP synthase.

Unit 4: Cell Communication & Cell Cycle

10–15%FRQ heavy
Big Ideas (CED)
  • Cells communicate using chemical signals and receptors.
  • Signal transduction pathways produce specific cellular responses.
  • Feedback mechanisms maintain homeostasis.
  • Cell cycle regulation controls growth and reproduction.
Cell Signaling
  • Reception: ligand binds a receptor.
  • Transduction: intracellular pathway relays/amplifies signal.
  • Response: gene expression, enzyme activation, secretion, movement, or division.
  • Signals may be direct contact, local/paracrine, autocrine, or endocrine.
  • Pathways often use phosphorylation cascades and second messengers.
Feedback & Homeostasis
  • Negative feedback: reduces stimulus and stabilizes system.
  • Positive feedback: amplifies response until an endpoint.
  • Disrupted feedback can cause disease or loss of homeostasis.
Cell Cycle & Cancer
  • Cell cycle: G1 → S → G2 → M.
  • Checkpoints prevent damaged cells from dividing.
  • Cyclins and CDKs regulate progression.
  • Apoptosis removes damaged cells.
  • Cancer can result from mutations in proto-oncogenes, oncogenes, or tumor suppressor genes.
Common Mistakes
  • Forgetting that receptor shape determines ligand binding.
  • Writing “signal happens” without describing reception → transduction → response.
  • Confusing positive feedback with “good” and negative feedback with “bad.”
FRQ Tip: For signaling, explain the pathway as a chain: signal → receptor → relay/amplification → response.

Unit 5: Heredity

8–11%Probability + meiosis
Big Ideas (CED)
  • Meiosis produces genetically diverse haploid gametes.
  • Patterns of inheritance can be predicted using probability.
  • Chromosomal behavior explains many inheritance patterns.
Meiosis & Genetic Variation
  • Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes.
  • Meiosis II separates sister chromatids.
  • Crossing over occurs in prophase I and creates recombinant chromosomes.
  • Independent assortment occurs when homologous pairs align randomly.
  • Random fertilization increases variation.
  • Nondisjunction can cause abnormal chromosome numbers.
Inheritance Patterns
  • Dominant/recessive inheritance.
  • Incomplete dominance and codominance.
  • Multiple alleles and polygenic traits.
  • Sex-linked traits, especially X-linked recessive patterns.
  • Linked genes are inherited together more often unless crossing over separates them.
  • Environmental factors can influence phenotype.
Probability Skills
  • Product rule: probability of event A AND event B.
  • Sum rule: probability of event A OR event B.
  • Use Punnett squares, pedigrees, and probability reasoning.
  • Use recombination frequency to infer relative gene distance.
Common Mistakes
  • Confusing homologous chromosomes with sister chromatids.
  • Assuming all genes assort independently.
  • Forgetting that phenotype can be affected by environment.
FRQ Tip: For heredity questions, explain how chromosome movement in meiosis produces the observed inheritance pattern.

Unit 6: Gene Expression & Regulation

12–16%HIGH YIELD
Big Ideas (CED)
  • DNA stores heritable information.
  • Gene expression links genotype to phenotype.
  • Regulation controls when and how genes are expressed.
  • Biotechnology uses molecular tools to manipulate and analyze DNA.
DNA & Replication
  • DNA is antiparallel and complementary.
  • A pairs with T; G pairs with C.
  • Replication is semi-conservative.
  • DNA polymerase builds new strands and proofreads.
Gene Expression
  • Transcription: DNA → RNA.
  • RNA processing: eukaryotic mRNA may be capped, polyadenylated, and spliced.
  • Translation: mRNA codons determine amino acid sequence at ribosomes.
  • tRNA anticodons pair with mRNA codons.
  • Protein sequence affects folding and function.
Gene Regulation
  • lac operon: inducible; active when lactose is present and glucose is low.
  • trp operon: repressible; turned off when tryptophan is abundant.
  • Eukaryotes regulate transcription using transcription factors and chromatin structure.
  • Epigenetic changes affect gene expression without changing DNA sequence.
Mutations & Genetic Change
  • Silent, missense, nonsense, insertion, deletion, frameshift.
  • Mutation effects depend on whether protein structure/function changes.
  • Mutations are a source of genetic variation.
  • Viruses and plasmids can transfer genetic information between cells.
Biotechnology
  • PCR amplifies DNA.
  • Gel electrophoresis separates DNA fragments by size.
  • Restriction enzymes cut DNA at specific sequences.
  • Transformation introduces DNA into cells.
  • CRISPR can edit DNA sequences.
FRQ Tip: For mutation questions, write the chain: DNA change → mRNA/codon change → amino acid/protein change → phenotype.

Unit 7: Natural Selection

13–20%MOST IMPORTANT
Big Ideas (CED)
  • Evolution is change in allele frequencies in populations over time.
  • Natural selection acts on phenotypes but changes allele frequencies.
  • Evolution explains both unity and diversity of life.
  • Phylogenies represent hypotheses about evolutionary relationships.
Evidence for Evolution
  • DNA and protein sequence similarities.
  • Shared anatomical structures and embryological patterns.
  • Fossil evidence and transitional forms.
  • Biogeography and common ancestry.
Mechanisms of Evolution
MechanismHow it changes populations
MutationIntroduces new alleles.
Natural selectionIncreases alleles linked to higher reproductive success in a specific environment.
Genetic driftRandom allele frequency change, strongest in small populations.
Gene flowMovement of alleles between populations.
Nonrandom matingChanges genotype frequencies.
Natural Selection & Fitness
  • Fitness = reproductive success, not physical strength.
  • Selection requires variation, heritability, and differential survival/reproduction.
  • Directional, stabilizing, and disruptive selection affect trait distributions differently.
  • Environment determines which traits are advantageous.
Hardy–Weinberg
p + q = 1
p² + 2pq + q² = 1
  • Used as a null model for no evolution.
  • Conditions: large population, random mating, no mutation, no migration, no natural selection.
  • If observed values differ from expected values, evolution may be occurring.
Speciation, Extinction & Phylogeny
  • Speciation occurs when populations become reproductively isolated.
  • Prezygotic barriers prevent fertilization; postzygotic barriers reduce hybrid success.
  • Allopatric speciation involves geographic isolation; sympatric speciation occurs without geographic separation.
  • Phylogenetic trees show common ancestry; nodes represent common ancestors.
  • Extinction reduces biodiversity and can alter ecosystems.
FRQ Tip: Never say an individual evolves. Say allele frequencies change in the population because individuals with certain traits survive and reproduce more.

Unit 8: Ecology & Biodiversity

10–15%Data + graphs
Big Ideas (CED)
  • Organisms respond to environmental changes.
  • Energy flows through ecosystems while matter cycles.
  • Population and community interactions affect ecosystem structure.
  • Biodiversity influences ecosystem stability.
Responses to the Environment
  • Organisms use behavioral, physiological, and developmental responses to environmental changes.
  • Responses can improve survival and reproductive success.
  • Timing and magnitude of responses may be influenced by signals and feedback.
Population Ecology
  • Exponential growth: rapid growth under ideal conditions.
  • Logistic growth: growth slows as population approaches carrying capacity.
  • Carrying capacity: maximum population size an environment can support.
  • Density-dependent factors include competition, predation, disease, and resources.
  • Density-independent factors include weather, natural disasters, and seasonal events.
Community Ecology
  • Interactions include competition, predation, herbivory, parasitism, mutualism, and commensalism.
  • Niche partitioning can reduce competition.
  • Keystone species have large effects on community structure.
  • Invasive species can disrupt ecosystems due to reduced predators or competition.
  • Succession changes community composition over time.
Energy Flow & Productivity
  • Energy flows through trophic levels and is lost as heat.
  • Only a fraction of energy transfers to the next trophic level.
  • Primary productivity measures energy captured by producers.
  • Food chains and food webs model energy movement.
Matter Cycles
  • Carbon cycle: photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, combustion.
  • Nitrogen cycle: fixation, nitrification, assimilation, ammonification, denitrification.
  • Water cycle: evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration.
Biodiversity & Ecosystem Stability
  • Higher biodiversity can increase resilience after disturbance.
  • Loss of biodiversity can reduce ecosystem services and stability.
  • Human activities can alter habitats, nutrient cycles, climate, and species interactions.
FRQ Tip: Ecology FRQs often require graph interpretation. Describe the trend, identify the cause, and explain the biological consequence.

Quantitative Practice

Must practiceCommon FRQ skill
Hardy-Weinberg
p + q = 1
p² + 2pq + q² = 1
Practice: 9% show a recessive trait. Find q, p, and percent heterozygous.
q² = 0.09, q = 0.30, p = 0.70, 2pq = 0.42 = 42% heterozygous.
Chi-Square
χ² = Σ [(O - E)² / E]
df = categories - 1
Practice: Expected 3:1 in 100 offspring. Observed: 80 dominant, 20 recessive. Calculate χ².
Expected 75 and 25. χ² = 25/75 + 25/25 = 1.33. df = 1. If critical value is 3.84, fail to reject the null.
Graph Analysis & Rates
rate = Δy / Δx
Practice: O₂ production: 0 min = 0 mL, 2 min = 10 mL, 4 min = 25 mL. Calculate rates.

FRQ Practice Center

Write to score pointsCED-style prompts
FRQ formula: Answer the exact verb. Describe = what happens. Explain = why/how it happens. Justify = connect evidence to claim. Predict = state expected outcome. Calculate = show setup, answer, and units when needed.
Auto-feedback: Type a response, then click Check my FRQ. The dashboard looks for AP-style evidence: vocabulary, mechanism, prediction/claim, and justification. This is not a replacement for teacher scoring, but it helps students revise before submitting.
How to Score More FRQ Points
  • Label responses clearly as a, b, c, d.
  • Use biological vocabulary: allele frequency, active site, concentration gradient, signal transduction, carrying capacity.
  • Do not just name a process; explain the mechanism.
  • When data are provided, cite the data directly.
  • For experimental design, include independent variable, dependent variable, control, constants, and expected result.

FRQ 1: Enzymes & Energetics

A graph shows enzyme activity increasing from 10°C to 37°C and decreasing sharply above 45°C.

  1. Describe the trend shown in the graph.
  2. Explain why activity decreases at high temperature.
  3. Predict the effect of changing pH far from the optimum.
  4. Justify your prediction using protein structure.
Scoring guide: Award points for identifying the optimum temperature, explaining denaturation/changed active site, predicting reduced activity at nonoptimal pH, and connecting disrupted bonds/tertiary structure to reduced substrate binding.

FRQ 2: Cellular Respiration Data

A researcher measures oxygen consumption in germinating seeds at 10°C, 22°C, and 35°C.

  1. Identify the dependent variable.
  2. Explain why oxygen consumption can be used as a measure of cellular respiration.
  3. Predict which temperature will likely show the highest respiration rate.
  4. Justify your prediction using enzyme activity and metabolism.
Scoring guide: DV = oxygen consumption/rate. Oxygen is final electron acceptor in the ETC. Prediction should be supported by temperature effects on enzymes; extreme temperatures may reduce activity by disrupting protein structure.

FRQ 3: Photosynthesis Experiment

Students use floating leaf disks to test photosynthesis rate under different light intensities.

  1. Identify the independent variable.
  2. Identify one appropriate control.
  3. Explain why disks float as photosynthesis occurs.
  4. Predict how increasing light intensity affects the rate until another factor becomes limiting.
Scoring guide: IV = light intensity. Control could be dark/no light or constant CO₂/temperature. Disks float because O₂ accumulates in leaf tissue. Rate increases until another limiting factor such as CO₂ or enzyme capacity limits photosynthesis.

FRQ 4: Cell Transport & Osmosis

A plant cell is placed in a hypertonic solution.

  1. Describe the direction of net water movement.
  2. Explain the effect on the plant cell.
  3. Predict the long-term outcome if the cell remains in the solution.
  4. Justify using membrane properties and water potential/osmosis.
Scoring guide: Water leaves the cell toward the higher solute concentration. The cell loses turgor pressure and plasmolyzes. Explanation should include selectively permeable membrane and osmosis down water potential gradient.

FRQ 5: Cell Communication

A mutation changes the shape of a membrane receptor involved in a hormone signaling pathway.

  1. Describe the normal sequence of a signal transduction pathway.
  2. Explain how the receptor mutation could affect the pathway.
  3. Predict one possible cellular response if the ligand cannot bind.
  4. Justify your prediction using structure-function reasoning.
Scoring guide: Normal pathway: reception, transduction, response. Altered receptor shape can prevent ligand binding or activation. Cellular response may be reduced/absent because signal is not transmitted or amplified.

FRQ 6: Cell Cycle & Cancer

A mutation in a tumor suppressor gene prevents a checkpoint protein from functioning.

  1. Describe the role of checkpoints in the cell cycle.
  2. Explain how loss of checkpoint function can lead to cancer.
  3. Predict how apoptosis could affect damaged cells.
  4. Justify why mutations in regulatory genes can have large effects.
Scoring guide: Checkpoints prevent division of damaged cells. Loss allows uncontrolled division and mutation accumulation. Apoptosis eliminates damaged cells. Regulatory genes affect many downstream cell cycle events.

FRQ 7: Meiosis & Heredity

Two genes are located on the same chromosome. Offspring phenotypes do not match the expected independent assortment ratio.

  1. Explain why linked genes may not assort independently.
  2. Describe how crossing over can produce recombinant offspring.
  3. Predict how distance between genes affects recombination frequency.
  4. Justify your prediction.
Scoring guide: Linked genes are inherited together because they are on the same chromosome. Crossing over can separate alleles. Farther apart genes have higher recombination frequency because crossovers are more likely between them.

FRQ 8: Gene Expression & Mutation

A point mutation changes a codon in a gene that codes for an enzyme.

  1. Describe transcription and translation.
  2. Explain how the mutation could affect protein function.
  3. Predict one possible phenotypic effect.
  4. Justify using structure-function reasoning.
Scoring guide: Transcription makes mRNA from DNA; translation uses codons to assemble amino acids. Mutation may change amino acid sequence, folding, active site, and enzyme function; phenotype depends on protein role.

FRQ 9: Gene Regulation

Bacteria are grown in a medium containing lactose but no glucose.

  1. Predict whether the lac operon will be active.
  2. Explain the role of lactose/allolactose in regulation.
  3. Explain why absence of glucose increases lac operon transcription.
  4. Justify how this regulation benefits the cell.
Scoring guide: lac operon active. Allolactose binds repressor, preventing it from blocking transcription. Low glucose increases cAMP-CAP activation. This allows efficient lactose metabolism only when useful.

FRQ 10: Hardy-Weinberg

In a population of 500 individuals, 45 individuals show a recessive phenotype.

  1. Calculate q².
  2. Calculate q and p.
  3. Calculate the expected frequency of heterozygotes.
  4. Identify one condition required for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.
Scoring guide: q² = 45/500 = 0.09. q = 0.30. p = 0.70. 2pq = 2(0.70)(0.30) = 0.42. Conditions include no selection, no mutation, no migration, large population, or random mating.

FRQ 11: Natural Selection

A bacterial population becomes increasingly resistant to an antibiotic after repeated exposure.

  1. Explain how natural selection leads to resistance.
  2. Describe the role of genetic variation.
  3. Predict the change in allele frequency over time.
  4. Justify using evolutionary principles.
Scoring guide: Resistant variants survive/reproduce more under antibiotic pressure. Variation may arise by mutation. Resistance allele frequency increases because resistant individuals have higher fitness in that environment.

FRQ 12: Ecology + Evolution

A drought reduces available food and water in an ecosystem. A small mammal population declines sharply.

  1. Explain how carrying capacity changes.
  2. Describe how population growth is affected.
  3. Predict how natural selection may act on the population.
  4. Justify using ecology and evolution together.
Scoring guide: Carrying capacity decreases due to reduced resources. Population growth slows or becomes negative. Traits improving survival/reproduction during drought may increase. Ecology creates selection pressure that drives evolutionary change.

FRQ 13: Chi-Square Genetics

A monohybrid cross is expected to produce a 3:1 phenotype ratio. The observed offspring are 80 dominant and 20 recessive.

  1. Calculate the expected numbers for 100 offspring.
  2. Calculate χ².
  3. Identify degrees of freedom.
  4. Explain whether the results support the expected ratio if the critical value is 3.84.
Scoring guide: Expected 75 and 25. χ² = (80-75)²/75 + (20-25)²/25 = 1.33. df = 1. Since 1.33 < 3.84, fail to reject the null; results are consistent with expected ratio.

FRQ 14: Cladograms & Biodiversity

A cladogram shows species A and B sharing a more recent common ancestor with each other than with species C.

  1. Identify which two species are most closely related.
  2. Explain what a node represents.
  3. Describe how shared derived traits are used in cladograms.
  4. Justify why DNA sequence comparisons can support evolutionary relationships.
Scoring guide: A and B are most closely related. A node represents a common ancestor. Shared derived traits define clades. More similar DNA sequences suggest more recent common ancestry.

AP Bio Scoring Skills: How to Turn Knowledge Into Points

4 → 5 skillsFRQ + data
Why this section matters: Many students know biology but lose points because they do not describe data, answer the command term, justify claims, or connect mechanisms. This section trains the skills AP readers reward.
1. Command Terms: Answer the Verb
CommandWhat it meansStudent move
DescribeSay what happens.State the pattern, process, or observation clearly.
ExplainSay why or how.Give a biological mechanism.
PredictSay what will happen.Give a specific expected outcome.
JustifyUse evidence/reasoning.Connect data or scenario details to the claim.
CalculateUse numbers.Show setup, answer, and units when needed.
IdentifyName it.Give the exact term or variable.
Common error: Students answer “what” when the prompt asks “why.” If the verb is explain, a mechanism is required.
2. Data Analysis Bootcamp

Graph Reading Formula

As X changes from ___ to ___, Y changes from ___ to ___, which suggests ___ because ___.
  • Line graph: focus on trend and slope/rate.
  • Bar graph: compare groups using values.
  • Error bars: overlapping bars may suggest differences are not clearly supported.
  • Table: look for patterns, maximum/minimum values, and changes over time.
Practice: Enzyme activity increases from 10 units at pH 5 to 30 units at pH 7, then decreases to 12 units at pH 9. Write a data-based claim.
Enzyme activity increases from pH 5 to pH 7, then decreases at pH 9, suggesting the enzyme has an optimal pH near 7 because pH changes can alter protein structure and active site function.
3. Experimental Design Trainer

Experimental Design Template

PartWhat to write
HypothesisIf the independent variable changes, then the dependent variable will change because...
Independent variableThe factor being changed.
Dependent variableThe measured result.
Control groupThe comparison group without the experimental treatment.
ConstantsFactors kept the same.
ReplicationRepeated trials increase reliability.
Practice: Design an experiment to test how pH affects enzyme activity.
Change pH levels as the independent variable and measure reaction rate as the dependent variable. Use the same enzyme concentration, substrate concentration, temperature, and reaction time. Include repeated trials at each pH. A control could use the enzyme at its known optimal pH or a reaction without enzyme for comparison.
4. What a Point-Earning Answer Looks Like
PromptWeak answerPoint-earning answer
Explain why enzyme activity decreases at high temperature.The enzyme stops working.High temperature disrupts bonds that maintain the enzyme’s tertiary structure, altering the active site and reducing substrate binding.
Explain antibiotic resistance.The bacteria get used to it.Bacteria with resistance alleles survive and reproduce more, increasing the frequency of resistance alleles in the population.
Explain water movement in a hypertonic solution.Water leaves.Water moves out of the cell by osmosis because the surrounding solution has lower water potential due to higher solute concentration.
5. Mixed Graph + Math Practice
Rate Practice: Product increases from 12 units at 2 minutes to 36 units at 6 minutes. Calculate rate and interpret biologically.
Rate = (36 − 12) / (6 − 2) = 24 / 4 = 6 units/min. Biologically, the reaction is producing product at an average rate of 6 units per minute during that interval.
Chi-square Practice: Expected 75 dominant and 25 recessive. Observed 80 dominant and 20 recessive. Interpret χ² = 1.33 if critical value = 3.84.
Because 1.33 is less than 3.84, fail to reject the null hypothesis. The observed results are not significantly different from the expected 3:1 ratio.

7. Common Misconceptions
  • Individuals do not evolve; populations evolve.
  • Energy is transformed and transferred, not created.
  • Plants perform cellular respiration.
  • Fitness means reproductive success.
  • Enzymes do not change equilibrium or ΔG.
  • Water moves toward lower water potential / higher solute concentration.
  • Dominant does not mean more common or more beneficial.
8. Lab-Based Thinking
  • Diffusion/osmosis: predict mass change based on solute gradients.
  • Enzymes: measure reaction rate under different pH, temperature, or substrate levels.
  • Photosynthesis: use oxygen production or floating leaf disks as evidence of rate.
  • Respiration: use oxygen consumption or CO₂ production as evidence.
  • Genetics: use chi-square to evaluate whether observed data fit expected ratios.
Experimental flaw question: Look for missing control, small sample size, no replication, uncontrolled variables, or unclear dependent variable.

  • p = frequency of dominant allele
  • q = frequency of recessive allele
  • p² = homozygous dominant, 2pq = heterozygous, q² = homozygous recessive

χ² = Σ [(O − E)² / E] df = categories − 1

Water Potential

Ψ = Ψs + Ψp Ψs = −iCRT
  • Ψ = water potential
